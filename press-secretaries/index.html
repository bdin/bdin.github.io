Sean Spicer’s stint as press secretary unusually short
By Benjamin Din
July 22, 2017

At six months, Sean Spicer’s tenure as White House press secretary was not very long.

On Friday, Spicer resigned from his post in response to President Donald Trump tapping financier Anthony Scaramucci as White House communications director, despite Spicer’s protests. His deputy, Sarah Huckabee Sanders, was announced as his replacement later that day.

But was it the shortest in history? Close, but not quite. Compared to Spicer’s 182 days, five other press secretaries have served between 19 and 111 days.

It is not uncommon for presidents to have multiple press secretaries. Barack Obama had three over two terms, and George W. Bush had four. When you factor in the timing of Spencer’s resignation, however, things start to look different.

Trump is only halfway through the first year of his term, and he’s already on his second press secretary.

That’s only happened twice, under Ronald Reagan and Gerald Ford. Reagan’s press secretary, James Brady, was shot in the head during an assassination attempt on the president, which left Brady permanently disabled. Ford’s pick, Jerald terHorst, resigned after Ford pardoned Richard Nixon for his involvement in Watergate.

Two other press secretaries – Harry Truman’s Roger Tubby and Bill Clinton’s Jake Siewert – couldn’t have served longer, even if they wanted to. Both were appointed by lame-duck presidents at the end of their terms.

Under Franklin Roosevelt, Jonathan Daniels served for just 19 days, replacing Stephen Early, the longest-serving press secretary at more than 12 years. Roosevelt’s unexpected death led to Daniels being replaced soon after.

Although Spicer can not claim the title of briefest stint, he does come fairly close – especially considering the extreme circumstances that some of his peers faced.
